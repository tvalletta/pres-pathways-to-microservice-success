Pathways to

Microservice

Success

Microservice Architecture is not a technological breakthrough that renders all previous architectural approaches obsolete. Is is just a set of trade-offs that, given the right discipline, improve the scalability and maintainability of complex systems.
Pros Cons
Scaling Availability
Workload Partitioning Operational Complexity
Release Independence Release Coordination
Polyglot Development Dependencies
Ownership & Accountability Debugging Distributed Systems

Scale

  • Scaling a monolith comes down to scaling the worst performing peice
  • Scaling an individual service based on utilization makes sense
  • Auto scaling is more effective with smaller pieces
 

Availability

  • It is easier to keep one app running than 10/100/1000 services
  • Microservices require discipline in testing, monitoring and alerting
  • Microservices require a DevOps culture
 

Workload Partitioning

  • Decreased complexity/cognitive load
  • Smaller builds/test suites and shorter deploy times
  • Less risk for experimentation/innovation
  • Isolate impact of updating platform and dependencies
  • More "new" projects
 

Operational Complexity

  • Its a distributed system
  • Requires mature tooling - Usually containerization and orchestration
  • Requires a mature deployment and integration model - Requires test confidence*
  • Microservices require discipline in testing, monitoring and alerting
 

Release Independence

  • Independent versioning
  • Independent validation
  • Independent release
  • Continous delivery
 

Release Coordination

  • Thoughtful versioning schemes required
  • Coordinated feature flagging required
 

Polyglot

  • Microservices can use any technology that can communicate
  • Best of breed
  • Good for recruiting
  • Good for innovation
 

Development Dependencies

  • Difficult to manage lots of dependent services
  • Requires tooling to make development enjoyable
  • Significantly reduces build times and test run times
 

Ownership & Accountability

  • Monoliths require hand-offs and specialization
  • Microservice teams can own all aspects of delivery
  • Always know who to call
 

Debugging

  • Debugging distributed systems can be difficult
  • Requires tooling to make debugging possible
 
Now that we know that microservice architecture is the destination, what are the steps that we need to take to successfully achieve our goal?
Devops Culture
Developer and operation roles need to reside on the same team in order to support and maintain several services. DevOps culture decreases time to production and increases autonomy.
*Research shows DevOps improves uptime
Autonomous Teams
Teams must have all the skills required to support the development, testing, monitoring and operationalization of their service(s). They are responsible and accountable.
Team Owned Uptime
Teams monitor their services. The team or a designated member of the team gets alerted it is not operating as designed.
Full Stack Teams
A team has all the skills required to support the development, testing, monitoring and operationalization of their service(s) even when people go on vacation.
Full-stack teams ->
    Team owned uptime

Continuous Delivery
Frequent small releases allow for greater velocity and a reduced blast radius for issues. As more services are added to an ecosystem coordinating large, distributed code releases becomes
impossible.
Test Confidence
In order to continuously deploy code you must have gates that prevent bad code from being promoted. You cannot trust your deployment gates if you do not trust your tests.
Test Coverage
To have confidence that your tests will catch bugs, you must have good tests for every feature. You need 100% feature coverage with your tests.
Rigorous Code Reviews
Coverage for coverage's sake erodes confidence by introducing tests that waste time without catching bugs. Rigorous code reviews can foster ownership, cross-train team members and identify untested features.
Better code reviews ->
    Test confidence

Development Flags
Team members do normal development behind a feature flag. Flags are managed by a service so that they can be easily depricated and removed.

Container Orchestration
Microservice architectures attempt to simplify development through modularity but do this throug increasing operational complexity. Container orchestration platforms like kubernetes provide tooling that efficiently manages the operational complexity.
Containerization
Teams leverage containers to encapsulate complexity and leverage automation. Containers are easily run and shared. Many of the costs of polyglot development are mitigated by containers.
Development Containers
Teams leverage containers in development to reduce onboarding of new team members and to easily switch between several projects. Development environments more closely match production.
Container Skills
Team members use containers for all stages of service development. They have the relevant skill and understanding to do so.
Containerization ->
    Dev to Prod Consistency
